Reading Passage: The Rise of Urban Farming

In recent years, urban farming has gained significant popularity across many cities worldwide. Once considered an unusual hobby, it is now a practical solution for addressing issues such as food insecurity, climate change, and urban unemployment.

Urban farming refers to the practice of cultivating, processing, and distributing food in or around urban areas. It includes rooftop gardens, vertical farms, community plots, and hydroponic systems that do not require soil. These methods make it possible to grow food in limited spaces using less water and energy than traditional agriculture.

One of the key benefits of urban farming is its environmental impact. Growing food locally reduces the need for long-distance transportation, thus cutting greenhouse gas emissions. Moreover, green spaces help reduce urban heat islands—areas that are significantly warmer than surrounding regions due to human activities and infrastructure.

Urban farms also promote community development. They can create jobs, especially for marginalized communities, and offer educational opportunities. For example, students can learn biology and sustainability by growing vegetables at school. Elderly individuals can also benefit from gardening, as it offers physical activity and social interaction.

Despite the advantages, urban farming faces challenges. Land availability is a major issue in densely populated cities. Additionally, obtaining permits and ensuring compliance with city regulations can be time-consuming. There is also a lack of widespread knowledge about advanced farming techniques among urban residents.

Nevertheless, the trend is expected to grow. With increasing awareness about healthy eating and sustainable living, urban farming may become a common feature of modern cities, offering both environmental and social rewards.
